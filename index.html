40% fewer abandoned applications. 85% reduction in council support calls. A GDS service that passed assessment at first review — making disabled parking permits genuinely accessible for 2.5 million eligible users.

01 · The Problem

The paper trail was failing the people who needed help most

The Blue Badge scheme provides parking concessions to around 2.5 million disabled people in England. Yet the existing process was paper-heavy, inaccessible, and deeply frustrating — applicants were searching outdated leaflets, travelling to photo booths, waiting at GP surgeries, and manually posting documents into a six-week black hole with no status updates.

Historically, 20% of applications were lost in the mail or rejected due to unclear handwriting. The existing digital service was riddled with WCAG failures and so confusing that many eligible applicants gave up entirely.

"I tried to apply online but it kept asking me questions I didn't understand. I gave up after 20 minutes and called the council instead. They told me I'd have to start again."— Blue Badge applicant, discovery research

As-Is vs To-Be service map — four pain points in the paper process mapped against the digital solution's value adds at each stage.

Gerald Thompson (72) — severe arthritis, uses trackball mouse, prefers physical mail. Sarah Chen (28) — tech-savvy caregiver, frequent mobile user.

02 · Research

Understanding the people behind the application

Before any design work began, I ran a structured discovery phase: reviewing analytics to identify drop-off points, shadowing council phone support agents, and conducting contextual interviews with applicants across three eligibility categories — including participants with mobility impairments, visual impairments, and cognitive disabilities.

  • Screen reader failures: Users with visual impairments could not navigate existing forms — core failures on contrast, focus indicators, and screen reader labelling throughout.
  • Unclear evidence requirements: Applicants didn't know what documents were acceptable, why they were needed, or how to upload them.
  • Session timeouts losing all progress: The single most common reason for support calls and the biggest driver of abandoned applications.
03 · Ideation

GOV.UK Design System as the structural backbone

The GOV.UK question-per-page pattern was the structural foundation — each screen presenting only what was needed, progressively revealing complexity only when necessary. I mapped the full service architecture across all eligibility paths before sketching any screens.

The key decision: I chose the question-per-page pattern despite early stakeholder pressure to reduce 'the number of clicks.' The research was unambiguous — single focused questions reduce errors and cognitive load, particularly for users with limited digital confidence.

The inclusive user flow — annotated with GDS pattern references and WCAG decisions at each branching point.

  • Accessibility is not a checklist — it's a design philosophy. Embedding WCAG 2.2 AA from the first sketch produced a genuinely better experience for everyone.
  • Design for your hardest user first. Every decision was tested against Gerald — motor-impaired, low digital confidence. If it worked for Gerald, it worked for everyone.

01 · The Problem

A missing capability costing loyalty and revenue

Harrods had no pre-order capability. For limited-edition and high-demand products — where exclusivity and guaranteed access are core to the luxury proposition — this was a critical gap. High-intent customers were repeatedly missing launches, leading to frustration and missed conversion. The business was also unable to forecast demand for limited releases, making inventory management reactive rather than planned.

"I missed out on the limited-edition bag I wanted because I didn't know when it would be available. By the time I heard about it, everything was sold out. There should be a way to reserve items in advance."— Emma Thompson, Luxury Shopper, discovery research

Understanding the luxury mindset

Qualitative interviews with Harrods customers across segments, analytics-based journey mapping to identify friction and drop-off moments, and competitive analysis of luxury pre-order patterns. I anchored all design decisions around a high-value luxury shopper persona — time-poor, experience-led, and sceptical of anything that feels transactional.

  • Guaranteed access matters most: Customers value guaranteed access to limited releases over launch-day competition.
  • Reduced monitoring removes frustration: Pre-ordering removes the need for constant availability-checking.
  • Planning and budgeting in advance: High-value customers want to plan purchases ahead, not react to availability.

Phase 03 · AI Content Architecture

Each river profile required two distinct content modes — factual data and activity guidance. I engineered separate Claude prompts for each, then merged outputs into a single structured JSON object. This two-prompt approach prevented Claude from blending encyclopaedic and promotional tones, which early tests showed produced weaker content in both dimensions.

PROMPT A — Factual Layer: Generate a structured JSON profile for [RIVER_NAME] in the UK: - length_miles, length_km, origin, mouth, region, nations_passed_through - key_facts: array of 4 verified, interesting facts - wildlife_species: array of 6 species found in or near the river - conservation_status: SSSI | SAC | None - gold_deposits: boolean (historically documented only) PROMPT B — Activity Layer: For [RIVER_NAME], list 6 activities people do on or near this river. Each activity: { name, difficulty, best_section, permit_required: boolean } Output JSON only.

Phase 02 · Domain Selection

A Brand Domain, Not a Keyword Domain

"5metrescrum.com" was a deliberate brand-first decision — the name references the 5-metre scrum zone, instantly recognisable to any rugby player or coach, while creating a unique, ownable identity that no competitor could replicate. For a SaaS product, a memorable brand domain drives referral traffic, email deliverability, and trust in ways that exact-match domains cannot. The ".com" extension was essential given international user targets across Pro D2, Top 14, URC, and NPC competitions.

Phase 03 · AI-Driven Product Development

Building a SaaS with Claude as the Development Partner

Rather than framing Claude as a code generator, I approached it as a product co-engineer. Each feature was specified through a structured context prompt establishing the product's data model, existing component library, and UX constraints — before requesting implementation. This context-first prompting approach reduced iteration cycles by keeping Claude within the established system architecture.

SYSTEM CONTEXT (sent before every feature prompt): You are building 5Metre Scrum — a rugby player passport SaaS. Stack: React, Supabase (auth + database), Stripe (billing), Tailwind. Data model: players have profiles (position, height, weight, passport_country, bronco_time, video_urls[]), clubs have scout_access boolean. Design system: dark navy (#0a1628) primary, gold accent (#d4a017). Existing components: PlayerCard, MetricsChart, VideoReel, PassportBadge. FEATURE REQUEST: Build the ScoutBoard component. It should: - Fetch players from Supabase filtered by: position, passport_country, min_bronco_time - Display as a responsive grid of PlayerCards - Show a "Priority" badge on players with is_first_xv = true - Include a CSV export button for club admins

Key Learnings

  • Context-first prompting — establishing the full system architecture before each feature request — is the single highest-leverage practice in AI-assisted development.
  • Brand domains outperform keyword domains for SaaS. "5metrescrum.com" created memorability and word-of-mouth that no keyword-match domain could have generated.
  • Passport eligibility filtering was the platform's killer feature — identified through keyword research on "EU passport rugby player" before a single line of code was written.
  • Treating Claude as a product co-engineer (not just a code generator) requires investing in shared context — the more the model understands the system, the more coherent its outputs.

Phase 02 · Domain Selection

Exact-Match with Authoritative .co.uk

"ukrugbyclubdirectory.co.uk" is a deliberate exact-match strategy. The domain contains "rugby clubs" — the head term — "UK" for geographic scope, and "directory" for the categorical signal. For a directory site in a competitive niche, an exact-match .co.uk domain provides meaningful ranking support in early-stage growth before the backlink profile matures. I evaluated "rugbyclubs.co.uk" (taken) and "ukrugby.co.uk" (too broad) before landing on the current domain, which was available and optimal.

Phase 03 · AI Content Architecture

Generating 620 Club Profiles at Scale

620 clubs required a systematic data pipeline. I designed a two-stage Claude workflow: first, a research prompt that gathered club founding year, ground name, league tier, and known honours; second, a formatting prompt that produced consistent structured JSON ready for Astro's content collections. For clubs with limited public data, I engineered a graceful-degradation prompt that produced shorter but accurate profiles rather than hallucinating details.

STAGE 1 — Data Research Prompt: For [CLUB_NAME] rugby club in [REGION], UK, provide verified information: - founded_year (if known, else null) - ground_name and town - current_league and league_type: union | league - kit_colours: primary and secondary - notable_achievements: array of up to 3 verified facts - contact_url or website if available IMPORTANT: If you are not confident a fact is accurate, output null. Do not invent founding years, league positions, or achievements. Output JSON only. STAGE 2 — Profile Copy Prompt: Using this JSON data: [STAGE_1_OUTPUT] Write a 60-word club description for the UK Rugby Club Directory. Tone: welcoming, factual, community-focused. No superlatives. No invented claims.
Phase 04 · Build & Technical SEO

Resolving Canonical & Indexing Issues on Netlify SPA

The most technically complex challenge on this project was a Netlify SPA indexing problem: hash-fragment URLs (e.g. /#/clubs/bristol) were not being crawled, duplicate titles from Netlify Edge functions were causing duplicate content flags, and broken redirect loops were compounding the issue. I diagnosed and resolved all three using Netlify Edge Functions for server-side canonical injection, a custom redirect rules file, and Astro's static generation for club profile pages.

  1. Identified hash-fragment URL pattern preventing Googlebot from crawling individual club pages
  2. Migrated club profile routing from SPA hash-fragments to Astro static pages at clean URL paths (/clubs/bristol-rfc)
  3. Deployed Netlify Edge Function to inject correct canonical tags server-side before HTML reached the browser
  4. Audited and fixed 14 broken redirect loops using Netlify's _redirects file with 301 status codes
  5. Added XML sitemap generation covering all 620 club URLs for accelerated Googlebot discovery

Key Learnings

  • Graceful-degradation prompting — instructing Claude to output null rather than guess — is essential at scale. Hallucinated founding years or league positions in a public directory destroy user trust instantly.
  • SPA hash-fragment routing is an SEO anti-pattern. Astro's static generation at clean URL paths was the correct architectural decision and should be the default for any content-heavy directory.
  • Free club registration is a built-in link acquisition strategy — clubs naturally link to their own profiles, generating organic backlinks without any outreach effort.
  • Technical SEO problems (canonicals, redirect loops) are debugging challenges, not creative ones. Systematic diagnosis — one issue at a time — is the correct approach, and AI tools accelerate that process significantly.
